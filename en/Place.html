Kinkaku-ji / The golden Pavillon

The Kinkaku-ji, a century-old temple and major tourist monument

The Kinkaku-ji is a Buddhist temple located in the Kyoto area.

It is mainly known as one of the most visited tourist monuments over the years. Since the beginning of its construction in 1397, the golden pavilion has known many owners and many destructions...

Originally owned by the powerful Saionji Kintsune, the temple has changed ownership many times and has gradually become one of the major tourist sites in Japan.

From a villa for the famous Saionji Kintsune to a Zen temple directed by the son of the latter until 1397. Date when Yoshimitsu, important personality of the Japanese politics, decides to invest the place and to build his villa intended to welcome several Buddhist relics. At his death, he let his son Yoshimochi make of the Kinkaku-ji a Zen temple and this, faithfully to his last will. The temple was later burned down multiple times during the Ōnin War and only the famous golden pavilion survived.

In 1950, the temple was again set on fire by a mentally deficient monk who then tried to end his life.

Today, after many restorations, the temple is recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site among the historical monuments of ancient Kyoto. If the temple is so successful nowadays, it is because of its unique history and its singular architecture combining three different styles: The shinden-zukuri style for the first floor; The buke-zukuri style for the second floor, a style common with samurai houses; The Karayō style for the second floor, a style common with Zen temples.
